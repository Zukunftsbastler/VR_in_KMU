1. VR Environment and Scenario Design
The workshop was designed to simulate remote collaboration in Virtual Reality, specifically utilizing a Principal-Agent dilemma scenario. It was built using the Unity platform.
* Virtual Meeting Room: The environment features a glass box meeting room with a large screen displaying the current round and negotiation results. The external environment surrounding the glass box is interchangeable, allowing scenes like a co-working space or a galaxy in outer space.
* Seating Arrangement: Participants sit at an elliptical/round meeting table. The Principal (CEO) sits at the head of the table, while the two Agents sit opposite each other on the long sides, facing the presented data and offers.
* Game Mechanics: 
    * The scenario is a repeated game to test participant credibility, with a randomized, undisclosed number of rounds. There is a minimum of three rounds, after which the probability of a subsequent round decreases exponentially (dropping below 1% by the 7th round).
    * Each round lasts a maximum of 3 to 5 minutes, intentionally creating time pressure and cognitive overload to simulate realistic, imperfect decision-making conditions.
* Variables and Hidden Agendas:
    * Agents allocate a hypothetical budget across four changing input variables per round (e.g., personnel hours, machine hours).
    * These inputs generate public company outputs (e.g., revenue, ethics, future development) which the Principal evaluates.
    * Agents operate with an information advantage and a "Hidden Agenda". They are assigned random personas with specific private utility goals (e.g., desire for power, family time) linked to certain input variables.
    * Agents can score up to 10 points per private utility goal (max 30 points per round), but the final score depends on the acceptance of the opposing Agent's offer.
2. Participant Structure
A total of five experimental groups were conducted. One group failed rapidly during their first attempt due to confusion, leading to a repeated workshop which was then used for the final evaluation.
* Demographics: Participants were exclusively first-semester Business Administration Bachelor students recruited from a "Digital Economy" lecture.
* VR Experience: Participants had little to no prior VR experience; only one individual reported having worn a headset two years prior.
* Exceptions: In one instance, due to a missing participant, the supervising professor stepped in to play the role of the Principal (CEO).
* Motivation: Participation was voluntary and driven by technological curiosity, with no monetary compensation or impact on academic grades.
3. Workshop Procedure and Methodology
To ensure high immersion and prevent audio bleed, participants were physically separated during the VR phase.

1. Preparation Phase: Participants were gathered in an undisturbed room (either a separate lecture hall or a digital office).
2. Pre-Assessment: Participants completed a consent form, an ATI (Affinity for Technology Interaction) questionnaire with three questions, and the first part of an FMS (Fast Motion Sickness) scale.
3. Briefing and Role Distribution: The Principal-Agent theory and general scenario were explained. Roles were assigned quasi-randomly based on where participants chose to sit, as the role instructions were placed on the seats beforehand.
4. 1-on-1 VR Onboarding: Participants were individually taken to separate rooms where they were instructed on headset and controller usage before joining the virtual session.
5. Execution (God Mode): The experiment was overseen via a "Master Client" (God mode) which controlled the scenario, distributed roles, and recorded participant audio and movement within the VR space.
6. Post-Assessment: Following the VR session, participants completed the second part of the FMS scale and a UEQ-S (User Experience Questionnaire).
7. Qualitative Interview: A short, semi-open interview was conducted covering four main categories: initial gut feeling, comparison to traditional video calls, usability/work value, and transferability to practice (hurdles and scenarios).
4. Key Observations and Data Collection
* Physical Tracking: While a dedicated live observer was not available, data was extracted from the Master Client recordings. Observations included physical gestures and participants turning their bodies/heads toward the active speaker.
* Information Handling: Participants were highly secretive regarding their private utility points but surprisingly open with their input variables, often mistakenly assuming the Principal had access to that information.
* Motion Sickness: The seated setup generally prevented severe motion sickness, but one participant experienced significant nausea; they pushed through the workshop out of discipline without notifying the administrators until the end. A second participant also reported feeling worse post-experiment.
